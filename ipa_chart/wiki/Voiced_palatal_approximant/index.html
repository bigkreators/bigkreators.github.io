      Voiced post-palatal trill
      Voiced alveolo-palatal trill
      r

      The voiced palatal trill is a type of consonantal sound, potentially used in some languages. There is no symbol for it in the International Phonetic Alphabet although if necessary , a small capital version of the Latin letter j,[citation needed] or can be used. The voiced post-palatal trill is a retracted version of the voiced palatal trill, though it is unlikely that it occurs in any languages.

      In the IPA chart it is marked as possible.[1] However, the extIPA chart marks it impossible,[2] and in fact it is not strictly impossible, but very difficult to pronounce.[3]

      Some languages have the voiced alveolo-palatal trill. However in the languages that have it, it is not pure alveolo-palatal, but rather palatalized dental/alveolar. The symbol that represents the voiced palatalized alveolar trill is r and the alveolo-palatal one is represented as a laminal retracted version r or the retracted diacritic can be omitted r because r alone is dental, alveolar, and postalveolar.

      Features

      [edit source]
      • Its manner of articulation is trill, which means it is produced by directing air over an articulator so that it vibrates.
      • Its place of articulation is palatal, which means it is articulated with the middle or back part of the tongue raised to the hard palate.
      • Its phonation is voiced, which means the vocal cords vibrate during the articulation.
      • It is an oral consonant, which means air is allowed to escape through the mouth only.
      • It is a central consonant, which means it is produced by directing the airstream along the center of the tongue, rather than to the sides.
      • Its airstream mechanism is pulmonic, which means it is articulated by pushing air solely with the intercostal muscles and abdominal muscles, as in most sounds.

      Fricative trill

      [edit source]
      Voiced palatal fricative trill

      The fricative trill is the same, but with simultaneous frication, though like the voiced post-palatal trill, it is unlikely that it occurs in any languages.
